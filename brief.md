# Brief

**Client:** Me. **Problem owner:** Me.

I generate *hundreds* of markdown files a week — notes, drafts, research, half-ideas, AI outputs — and they all land in one undifferentiated heap. The problem isn't any single file; it's the **mountain.** One giant unsorted pile is paralyzing: I can't see what's there, so I deal with none of it, and it grows faster than I could ever file it by hand. Real work — the draft I owe, the idea worth building — gets buried under noise I never sorted, and I only notice at 3am.

I don't need it spotless. My real life runs on *organized mess* — clothes piled by type, dishes rinsed and stacked. Not done, but sorted *enough* that the real cleaning, when it comes, is easy. **Five labeled hills are climbable; one mountain isn't.**

**What I've tried.** Manual filing — too slow; at hundreds a week I never keep up and the heap wins. Note apps and tags — they store, they don't *sort*; the pile just becomes a searchable pile.

**What I need.** A folder-based operator that reads each md file *fast*, decides which **pile** it belongs in — against piles I define, proposing new ones when the files call for them — and moves it there. It keeps everything; files can move piles later as they evolve. The output isn't a clean desk — it's an **organized mess:** one heap turned into a handful of labeled, climbable piles, ready to actually clean when I am.
